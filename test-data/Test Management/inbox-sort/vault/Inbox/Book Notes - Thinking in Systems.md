# Book Notes — Thinking in Systems

**Author:** Donella H. Meadows
**Finished:** 2026-03-08

## Key Concepts

### Stocks and Flows

A stock is the foundation of any system — water in a bathtub, money in a bank account, features in a backlog. Flows are what change stocks: inflows add, outflows subtract. You cannot change a stock instantaneously. This is why rapid organizational change feels so hard — the stock of institutional knowledge, trust, and culture changes slowly.

### Feedback Loops

Balancing loops seek stability (thermostat, performance reviews). Reinforcing loops amplify change (compound interest, viral growth, technical debt). Most problems come from reinforcing loops that nobody noticed until they dominated the system.

### Leverage Points

The most powerful leverage point is the mindset or paradigm out of which the system arises. Changing the rules is easier than changing the paradigm but harder than changing the numbers.

## Applications

### At Work

- Our sprint velocity is a stock. Adding developers is an inflow, but onboarding friction is a balancing loop that limits growth. Explains why adding people to a late project makes it later (Brooks' Law).
- Technical debt is a reinforcing loop: shortcuts today create more shortcuts tomorrow because the codebase becomes harder to change cleanly.

### In Life

- Health is a stock with slow dynamics. One workout does nothing visible, but 100 workouts transform the system. The feedback delay makes it hard to stay motivated.
- Relationships are stocks too. Trust accumulates slowly and depletes fast. Asymmetric flows.

## Favorite Quote

"We can't control systems or figure them out. But we can dance with them." — Meadows
